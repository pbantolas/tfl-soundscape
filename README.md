# TFL Sounds

London's underground network is one of the most data-rich public systems in the world. Every few seconds, TfL publishes live arrival predictions for every train across every line. Hundreds of events per minute, each one a small fact about the city in motion.

I wanted to hear it.

---

**TFL Sounds** turns real-time London Underground arrival data into generative music. Each tube line plays a different instrument, each incoming train triggers a note, and the rhythm of the city becomes something you can actually listen to.

## The idea

I've always been interested in the gap between data and sensation. Transit networks are particularly interesting for this: they're vast, deterministic-ish, and have their own sense of tempo. Rush hour has a different texture than 11pm. A suspended line goes silent.

The question was: what does that actually sound like?

## How it works musically

Each of six lines (Victoria, Central, Piccadilly, Circle, District, Jubilee) is assigned a voice, a register, and a role:

- **Anchor** lines (District) hold low, slow notes that ground the harmony
- **Texture** lines (Central, Piccadilly, Jubilee) fill the mid-range
- **Spark** lines (Victoria, Circle) play higher, sparser hits

The rhythm isn't random. Each line uses a **Euclidean rhythm**, a pattern generated by evenly distributing a number of hits across a number of steps. It's the same principle behind a lot of West African and Middle Eastern percussion, and it tends to produce patterns that feel organic without being predictable. The rotation and hit density are different per line, so they interlock rather than clash.

**Energy** is the other interesting variable. When a line is busy (lots of arrivals coming in) its energy level rises and gradually decays with a half-life of about 12 seconds. This energy modulates:

- The filter frequency (brighter when active, warmer when quiet)
- The BPM (52 → 76, quiet hours to rush hour)
- The reverb wetness (spacious and distant when idle, close and present when dense)
- A layer of brown noise that shifts in character: a low rumble at rest, building toward a wider, more present presence as activity increases

The ambient noise layer was one of the more satisfying details. It adds something that feels like the actual physical texture of a station.

## Time travel

One thing I didn't expect to find interesting: scrubbing through the timeline.

The app buffers arrival history and lets you seek anywhere in that window. Scrubbing backward plays the city's rhythm in reverse. Ping-pong mode bounces the playhead back and forth automatically!

## Running it

Works out of the box, no API key needed for normal usage. If you hit rate limits, grab a free key from [api.tfl.gov.uk](https://api.tfl.gov.uk) and add it to `.env.local`:

```
VITE_TFL_APP_KEY=your-key-here
```

Then:

```bash
bun install
bun run dev
```
